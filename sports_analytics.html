.fig_counter <- 0

save_last_plot <- function(name,
                           folder = "figures",
                           width = 8,
                           height = 6,
                           dpi = 300){
  
  dir.create(folder, showWarnings = FALSE, recursive = TRUE)
  
  .fig_counter <<- .fig_counter + 1
  
  fig_number <- sprintf("Fig%02d", .fig_counter)
  
  file_path <- paste0(
    folder, "/",
    fig_number, "_",
    name,
    ".png"
  )
  
  ggsave(
    filename = file_path,
    plot = ggplot2::last_plot(),
    width = width,
    height = height,
    dpi = dpi
  )
  
  message("Saved: ", file_path)
}

Introduction

To this point, this project seeks to analyze boxer performance data across (male and female) boxers that could provide solid metrics that coaches and prospective boxers can use for: (i) scouting, (ii) performance improvement, (iii) and metrics that can be used to argue for higher boxer pay. The data for this project was obtained and scrubbed from the UFC Website and compiled on Kaggle; with fights in recent history being cataloged (1996 to 2024).

This study attempts to identify key predictors across UFC win conditions and if these predictors are consistent. From these chosen variables, the amount of knockdown attempts, takedown accuracy, striking accuracy, submission attempts, and control time were all significant predictors. However, submission attempts was only significant in the submission win condition which was expected. When examining the multinomial logistic model’s ability to predict win condition with the confusion matrix, there was no loss in accuracy.

The generalizability in this model prediction may be affected by the smaller sample pool that was utilized and low amount of win conditions. While certain win conditions like Submission and Unanimous decisions seemed to be predicted fairly well, there seems to also be issues in overall predictability. First off, choosing women may have limited the amount of win conditions observed across these matches. In total there was only 1 disqualification (DQ), 5 technical knockouts due to Doctor Stoppage (TKO – Doctor Stoppage), and 5 Majority Decision (Decision – Majority) rulings. This limited training of the model may explain the incorrect identification of these matches as Unanimous Decision. This leads to the second limitation which may stem from not enough variables to explain variance in these matches. While there may be some variables like submission amount and knockdowns that seem to have inherent relatability to these different win conditions, these span across win rulings causing this variability to range. Using the submission win as the example, while submissions were the lead prediction, there are not enough variables to differentiate it from the unanimous decision ruling which may explain why the other model incorrectly predicted Submission wins as a unanimous win condition. This inclusion of more match specific data or including a more varying measurements can help to explain the variance seen in these matches. To reiterate the important predictors from this model, having higher amount of takedown accuracy and higher amount of knockdowns during a match will increase the likelihood of a win across all conditions. Interestingly enough, this also presents striking accuracy as a negative effect on KO/TKO and Submission Win conditions; illustrating that more dynamic or aggressive moves that result in Knockdowns or submissions could be more important in a match. From this, merely connecting punches may not be enough and negatively impact the method of winning in a UFC match.

To summarize, this exploratory analysis into UFC fighter data provides more context into predictors needed to determine a win across the different methods of winning a match. This methodology provides a proof of concept that could be adapted to accommodate a wider sample size and enhance the predictability of these variables across matches, providing ample opportunity to examine or swap variables of interest to increase the predictive power of this model and the applicability of these findings to UFC coaches, players, and enthusiasts.
